# Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: Which Email Platform Wins in 2023?

Choosing between Mailchimp and ConvertKit can be tough when you're building your email list. Both are powerful tools for email marketing, but they cater to slightly different audiences. Mailchimp is a go-to for small businesses and e-commerce stores, while ConvertKit shines for creators like bloggers and podcasters. In this Mailchimp vs ConvertKit comparison, we'll break down pricing, features, automation, analytics, and more to help you decide.

This guide is based on real user experiences and official platform details as of 2023. Whether you're starting out or scaling up, we'll provide practical advice to test both.

## Pricing: Mailchimp vs ConvertKit Breakdown

Pricing is often the first hurdle. Here's how they stack up.

### Mailchimp Pricing
Mailchimp offers a forever-free plan for up to 500 contacts and 1,000 monthly sends. Paid plans start at $13/month (Essentials) for 500 contacts, scaling to $350+/month for larger lists (Premium). Features like advanced automation and A/B testing unlock at higher tiers. Overage fees apply if you exceed contact limits.

**Practical Tip:** If you're under 500 contacts, start free. Watch for send limits—upgrading mid-month can get pricey.

### ConvertKit Pricing
ConvertKit (now Kit) has a free plan for up to 300 subscribers (10,000 emails/month) with basic forms and broadcasts. Creator plan starts at $9/month (300 subs), scaling to $29/month for 1,000 subs. No send limits, but commerce features (subscriptions, products) add $29+/month. It's more predictable—no overages.

**Practical Tip:** ConvertKit's free plan suits solopreneurs. Calculate based on subscribers, not sends, for bloggers with irregular campaigns.

**Winner:** ConvertKit for creators with growing lists; Mailchimp for high-volume senders on a budget.

## Ease of Use: User Interface and Onboarding

### Mailchimp's Drag-and-Drop Editor
Mailchimp's interface is beginner-friendly with a visual email builder, pre-made templates, and mobile previews. Onboarding includes tutorials and a setup wizard. However, navigation can feel cluttered for advanced users.

**Practical Advice:** Import contacts via CSV easily. Use the content optimizer for subject lines—test it on your first campaign.

### ConvertKit's Creator-Focused Dashboard
ConvertKit emphasizes simplicity with a clean dashboard, visual automations builder, and subscriber tags. Onboarding guides you through sequences and landing pages. It's less overwhelming but lacks Mailchimp's template variety.

**Practical Advice:** Tag subscribers by behavior (e.g., 'opened welcome email') from day one for better segmentation.

**Winner:** Mailchimp for visual designers; ConvertKit for automation enthusiasts.

## Key Features Comparison

### Email Templates and Design
Mailchimp: 100+ responsive templates, custom HTML, dynamic content blocks. Great for product promotions.
ConvertKit: Fewer templates (focused on newsletters), but excellent for clean, text-heavy designs. Commerce add-ons for paid newsletters.

### Automation and Sequences
Mailchimp: Basic automations free (welcome series), advanced customer journeys on Premium. Trigger-based (e.g., abandoned cart).
ConvertKit: Unlimited automations on all paid plans. Visual builder for if/then sequences—ideal for nurture flows.

**Practical Tip:** In ConvertKit, set up a 7-email welcome sequence: Day 1 intro, Day 3 value, Day 7 offer. Track opens to refine.

### Landing Pages and Forms
Mailchimp: Basic pop-ups, forms, and landing pages (paid). Integrates with websites.
ConvertKit: Robust landing pages, opt-in incentives, and pre-built forms. Native checkout for digital products.

### Integrations
Both integrate with Zapier, Shopify, WordPress. Mailchimp edges with Google Workspace, Facebook ads. ConvertKit strong with podcast tools (e.g., Transistor).

### Analytics and Reporting
Mailchimp: Detailed reports on opens, clicks, revenue tracking.
ConvertKit: Subscriber activity feeds, revenue per subscriber, A/B testing on subjects.

**Practical Advice:** Use Mailchimp's revenue tracking for e-commerce ROI. In ConvertKit, monitor 'subscriber lifetime value' to justify tool costs.

## Pros and Cons

- E-commerce features (product recommendations)
- Large template library

**Cons:**
- Expensive at scale
- Automation limits on lower plans
- Steeper learning for tags/segments

### ConvertKit Pros
- Unlimited emails/automations
- Creator tools (broadcasts, paid newsletters)
- Better segmentation

**Cons:**n
- Fewer design templates
- No free advanced analytics
- Higher cost for commerce

## Who Should Choose Mailchimp?
Small businesses, e-commerce owners, agencies needing robust templates and integrations. If you send frequent promotions and have under 5,000 contacts, Mailchimp's ecosystem wins.

**Migration Tip:** Export lists from competitors via CSV. Test a small campaign first to check deliverability.

## Who Should Choose ConvertKit?
Bloggers, podcasters, course creators focused on relationships over sales blasts. Perfect for 300-10,000 subs with automation-heavy workflows.

**Migration Tip:** Rebuild sequences manually—ConvertKit's importer handles basics, but tag mapping saves time.

## Testing Both: Practical Steps
1. Sign up for free plans.
2. Import 50 test subscribers.
3. Build a welcome automation and send a broadcast.
4. Compare setup time and analytics after 1 week.
5. Check mobile rendering and deliverability (use GlockApps for tests).

Consider your goals: High-volume sales? Mailchimp. Long-term nurturing? ConvertKit.

## Conclusion
Mailchimp vs ConvertKit boils down to your needs—Mailchimp for versatile marketing, ConvertKit for creator automation. Neither is 'best' universally; test both free tiers. For most solopreneurs, start with ConvertKit's simplicity. Scaling e-com? Mailchimp's power scales better.
