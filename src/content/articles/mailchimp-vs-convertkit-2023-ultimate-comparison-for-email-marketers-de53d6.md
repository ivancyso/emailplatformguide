# Mailchimp vs ConvertKit: Which Email Marketing Platform Wins in 2023?

Choosing between Mailchimp and ConvertKit can be tough when you're building your email list and scaling your marketing efforts. Both are popular platforms, but they cater to slightly different audiences. Mailchimp is a go-to for small businesses and e-commerce stores with its all-in-one marketing tools, while ConvertKit shines for creators, bloggers, and solopreneurs focused on nurturing subscribers with personalized sequences.

In this head-to-head comparison, we'll break down pricing, features, ease of use, integrations, support, and more. Whether you're a beginner or scaling up, you'll get practical advice to decide which fits your needs. Let's dive in.

## Pricing Comparison

Pricing is often the first make-or-break factor. Both offer tiered plans based on subscriber count, but their structures differ.

### Mailchimp Pricing

Mailchimp has a generous free plan for up to 500 contacts and 1,000 monthly sends. Paid plans start with:
- **Essentials**: $13/month for 500 contacts (billed annually), scaling up to $350+ for larger lists. Includes basic automation and A/B testing.
- **Standard**: $20/month for 500 contacts, adds advanced automation, dynamic content, and time-based sends.
- **Premium**: $350/month for 10,000 contacts, with multivariate testing, single-step automation, and priority support.

Overage fees apply if you exceed limits, and pricing jumps significantly past 10,000 subscribers. It's cost-effective for small lists but can get pricey for high-volume senders.

### ConvertKit Pricing

ConvertKit offers a 14-day free trial but no perpetual free plan. Plans are:
- **Creator**: $9/month for up to 300 subscribers (annually), $15 monthly. Unlimited emails, basic automations.
- **Creator Pro**: $29/month for 1,000 subs, adds advanced segmentation, one-click upsells, and subscriber scoring.
- **Enterprise**: Custom pricing from $79/month for 3,000+ subs, with API access and dedicated support.

ConvertKit's pricing is straightforward—no send limits—and it's cheaper for creators with growing lists under 10,000. Practical tip: If you're under 300 subs, start with ConvertKit's trial to test without commitment.

**Winner**: Mailchimp for free starters; ConvertKit for unlimited sends on budget plans.

## Ease of Use and Interface

### Mailchimp's Drag-and-Drop Editor

Mailchimp's interface is beginner-friendly with a visual email builder. Drag blocks for text, images, buttons—no coding needed. The dashboard organizes campaigns, audiences, and automations intuitively. However, it can feel cluttered as features pile up.

Practical advice: Use templates from their 100+ library to launch fast. Mobile app is solid for on-the-go management.

### ConvertKit's Clean Creator Dashboard

ConvertKit prioritizes simplicity for solopreneurs. The visual builder is straightforward, with forms, landing pages, and sequences front-and-center. Tagging system replaces complex segments, making personalization easier.

It's less overwhelming than Mailchimp but lacks some visual flair. Tip: Enable the commerce add-on for easy product upsells in emails.

**Winner**: Mailchimp for visual beginners; ConvertKit for streamlined creator workflows.

## Key Features Breakdown

### Automation and Sequences

Mailchimp offers customer journeys with triggers like purchases or site visits. Classic automations handle welcome series and abandoned carts. Pro: Behavioral targeting. Con: Single-step limits on lower plans.

ConvertKit excels in sequences—automated email series triggered by tags or actions. Visual automation builder maps subscriber paths. Pro: Infinite sequences; Con: Less e-commerce focus.

Advice: For nurture sequences, ConvertKit's tagging (e.g., 'lead-magnet-downloader') automates better without lists.

### Audience Segmentation and Analytics

Mailchimp uses groups and tags for segments, with reports on opens, clicks, revenue. Heatmaps show engagement.

ConvertKit's tagging is superior—auto-apply based on behavior. Analytics include subscriber growth, revenue per subscriber, and churn prediction on Pro plans.

Tip: Export segments to CSV in both for external tools.

### Templates and Design

Mailchimp: 200+ responsive templates, custom themes.
ConvertKit: Fewer (50+), but highly customizable with commerce blocks.

### Forms and Landing Pages

Both build opt-ins easily. Mailchimp has popups/modals; ConvertKit's are conversion-optimized for creators with incentive previews.

## Integrations

Mailchimp connects to 300+ apps (Shopify, WordPress, Google Analytics) via native or Zapier.

ConvertKit integrates with 100+ (Gumroad, Teachable, WordPress) and Zapier. Strong for creator tools like Patreon.

Practical tip: Use Zapier bridges if direct integration lacks—both support it seamlessly.

## Customer Support and Resources

Mailchimp: 24/7 email/chat on paid plans, extensive help center, webinars.
ConvertKit: Email support (faster on higher plans), creator-focused academy, community forum.

Both score high (4.5+ on G2), but ConvertKit feels more personal for indie users.

### ConvertKit

## Who Should Choose Mailchimp?

- E-commerce stores (abandoned cart flows)
- Agencies managing multiple clients
- Beginners needing free entry

Start here if you're testing email marketing without budget.

## Who Should Choose ConvertKit?

- Bloggers/content creators
- Course sellers (upsells seamless)
- Those prioritizing automation over design

Ideal if monetizing audiences via newsletters.

**Migration Tip**: Both import CSVs easily. Export from one, map fields, and test a small batch first. Use Zapier for ongoing syncs.

## Conclusion

Mailchimp vs ConvertKit boils down to your goals. Mailchimp wins for versatile marketing with free access and e-com power, perfect for businesses. ConvertKit edges out for creators with unlimited sends, smart tagging, and affordability at scale.

Test both—Mailchimp's free plan and ConvertKit's trial cost nothing. Track metrics like open rates (aim 20-30%) and revenue per email post-switch.
